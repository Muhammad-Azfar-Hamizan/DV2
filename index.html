Malaysia's High Risk Weather

Exploring the heat, rain, haze, and UV that shape everyday life across all 16 states · 2005–2024

Step outside in Malaysia on any given day and you already know what the data is about to tell you. It is hot. It is humid. And if the clouds are gathering, it is about to rain.

Malaysia does not have four seasons. What it has is heat, rain, haze, and a sun that never takes a day off. Sitting just north of the Equator, Malaysia's climate has always been intense. But intense is no longer the right word. The heat is climbing. The rainfall is rising. And the risks behind both are not standing still.

This visualisation maps four dimensions of Malaysia's climate risk across a decade of real data. The weather here is not background noise. It is a risk. And it is one every Malaysian lives with every single day.

Section 02

Rising Heat: Temperature Trends 2014–2024

Picture March in Malaysia. Late afternoon. No cloud cover. The temperature reads 38°C. For the construction worker, the delivery rider, the farmer — this is not an unusual afternoon. It is just another working day.

Malaysia's heat has always been intense. But the past decade shows something beyond intensity. It shows a trend. The national average peak temperature has risen 2.8°C in ten years. And 2024 — the hottest year on record — got there without a single El Niño event to blame. The warming is no longer cyclical. It is structural.

Annual Absolute Peak Temperature Trend (2014–2024)
Line chart · average peak temperature across all states (°C) · hottest state recorded shown as dashed line
Source: Kaggle — Malaysia Weather Data (1996–2024)
34.3°C in 2014. 37.1°C in 2024. A rise of 2.8°C in ten years. The IPCC has warned that even a 1–2°C rise in average temperatures significantly increases the risk of heatstroke and cardiovascular collapse — particularly for outdoor workers and the elderly. Malaysia has already crossed that threshold in its hottest states. And 2024 broke the record without El Niño. The trend line shows no plateau.

2017 was the warning. The decade since has not cooled down. In 2017, temperatures spiked across nearly every month — peaks of 38–40°C that looked like outliers. They were not outliers. Section Summary

+2.8°C in ten years. Faster than the global average. No sign of reversing. What was a record in 2017 is the norm by 2024. But heat is only the first risk. The second arrives every monsoon season — and it is getting heavier.

Section 03

Rainfall & Flood Risk: When the Monsoon Overwhelms

December 2014. The rivers across Kelantan, Terengganu, and Pahang rose faster than anyone could evacuate. Over 500,000 people displaced. The worst flooding in decades.

December 2021. Shah Alam. Kuala Lumpur. Highways submerged. Neighbourhoods that had never flooded before — flooded.

2023. Johor. 40,000 evacuated.

Three floods. Ten years. Three times described as exceptional. The data says otherwise.

Section Summary

35% more rainfall in under a decade. Three major floods. Three records broken. The monsoon has always been heavy. What has changed is how heavy — and how often that weight becomes a disaster. Rising rainfall is only one part of what makes Malaysia's weather dangerous. The next risk arrives not from the sky above — but from across the border.

Section 04

Air Quality & Haze: The Crisis We Did Not Start

September 2019. The sky over Kuala Lumpur turned a dull, acrid orange. Schools closed. The Air Pollutant Index crossed 100. The cause was not anything happening in Malaysia. It was peatland fires burning in Kalimantan, Indonesia — hundreds of kilometres away. The smoke drifted north. And Malaysia breathed it for weeks.

This is Malaysia's most unpredictable weather hazard. Most years the air is manageable. But when Indonesia's dry season meets an El Niño year and the fires start, Malaysia has no ability to stop what is coming. We did not start the fires. But we breathe the smoke.

Annual Unhealthy Air Days (Air Pollutant Index above 100)
Line chart · count of days per year where Air Pollutant Index exceeded 100 · worst haze years annotated · 2005–2022
Source: Kaggle — Malaysia Air Pollution Index
In 2014, Malaysia recorded 119 days of unhealthy air — one in three days of the year The Riau fires drove Malaysia's Air Pollutant Index above 100 for a third of the year — the worst on record. The 2015 Indonesia peat fires followed at 103 days. The 2019 Kalimantan fires spiked to 90. Between peaks, Malaysia averages 20–50 unhealthy days per year. Not a crisis — but not clean air. And the next fire season is always one dry El Niño year away.

It should not take a pandemic to get there.
Section Summary

Malaysia does not start the fires. But Malaysia breathes the smoke. Of the four weather risks in this visualisation, haze is the one we have the least power to control. Every dry El Niño year carries the risk of another 2014. That vulnerability — to something entirely beyond our control — is a risk all its own. But Malaysia's high risk weather is not done yet. There is one more hazard. It has been there all along. And almost no one talks about it.

Section 05

UV Risk: Malaysia's Hidden Sun Hazard

Unlike heat, rain, or haze, UV leaves no visible warning. The sky can be blue. The air clean. The temperature mild. And the UV index can still be at 12 — classified as Extreme by the WHO — quietly burning unprotected skin in under 15 minutes and accumulating damage over years that the body cannot reverse. The WHO classifies UV index 6 and above as High risk. Malaysia's median across all states sits well above 10. There is no state, no season, no corner of this map where that risk is low. This is what it means to live this close to the Equator.

Almost every state records a median UV above 10 — all classified Extreme Selangor leads — median peak UV of 14. Perlis at 13. Sabah, Sarawak, Melaka, Perak, and Pahang all at 12. The national all-time record: UV 20, in Negeri Sembilan, May 2023. At that level, unprotected skin burns in under 10 minutes. Nowhere in Malaysia is UV a minor concern. Nowhere.

Section Summary

Malaysia's weather is not one risk. It is four — and they stack.

Temperatures up 2.8°C in a decade. Rainfall up 35% in under ten years. Haze arriving uninvited from across the border. UV classified as Extreme in every single state, every single day.

No Malaysian state escapes at least one of these. Most face several at once.

The data is not here to alarm. It is here to inform. Check the API before going outside during haze season. Stay indoors during peak heat in March, April, and May. Wear sunscreen — every day. Not just at the beach.

Malaysia's weather has always been demanding. The data shows it is becoming more so. Knowing that is where protection begins.
